Formalism

Hilbert space

A complex valued function $f$ is square integrable in a domain $D$ if $\int_D |f(x)|^2 \,dx$ exists. The Hilbert space consists of square integrable functions on $\mathbb{R}$. Wave functions live in the Hilbert space.

The inner product of two functions $f, g$ is defined as \begin{align} \langle f | g \rangle &= \int_D f^*(x) g(x) \,dx \\ \langle f | g \rangle &= \langle g | f \rangle^* \end{align} The norm is defined as: \begin{align} \|f\| = \sqrt{\langle f | f \rangle} \end{align} Schwarz inequality \begin{align} |\langle f | g \rangle| \leq \sqrt{\|f\|^2 \|g\|^2} \end{align}

Observables

The expectation of an observable $Q$ with corresponding linear operator $\hat{Q}$ is given by \begin{align} \langle Q \rangle = \langle f | \hat{Q} f \rangle \end{align} because $Q$ refers to a physical property, it is real: \begin{align} \langle Q \rangle &= \langle Q \rangle^* \\ \langle f | \hat{Q} f \rangle &= \langle \hat{Q} f | f \rangle \end{align} Such $\hat{Q}$ are called Hermitian. It can also be proved that \begin{align} \langle f | \hat{Q} g \rangle &= \langle \hat{Q} f | g \rangle \end{align}

Determinate States

For some values of $\Psi$ there may be complete determinacy in $Q$. This means \begin{align} q &= \langle Q \rangle \\ \langle (Q - q)^2 \rangle &= 0 \\ \langle \Psi | (Q - q)^2 \Psi \rangle &= 0 \\ \langle (Q - q) \Psi | (Q - q) \Psi \rangle &= 0 \\ (Q - q) \Psi &= 0 \\ \end{align} This is an eigenvalue problem, with the eigenvector as $\Psi$. The spectrum is all the collection of all eigenvalues. Eigenvectors are said to be degenerate if they share the eigenvalue.
The wave function $\Psi(x, t)$ of a particle can be written as a 2 dimensional inverse Fourier Transform on $\hat{\Psi}(\kappa, \omega)$: \begin{align} \Psi(x, t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{\Psi}(\kappa, \omega) e^{i\kappa x - i \omega t} \, d\kappa \, d\omega \label{fft} \end{align} Its derivatives are \begin{align} \pdv{\Psi(x, t)}{t} &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} - i\omega \hat{\Psi}(\kappa, \omega) e^{i\kappa x - i \omega t} \, d\kappa \, d\omega \\ \pdv[2]{\Psi(x, t)}{x} &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} - \kappa^2 \hat{\Psi}(\kappa, \omega) e^{i\kappa x - i \omega t} \, d\kappa \, d\omega \end{align} with the classical wave convention on the signs of $\kappa$ and $\omega$. The energy of a particle is given by: \begin{align} E = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V \end{align} For a photon: \begin{align} E &= h \nu= \hbar \omega \\ \end{align} Using de Broglie equation: \begin{align} p &= \frac{h}{\lambda} = \hbar \kappa \\ \end{align} Combining the above results: \begin{align} \hbar \omega &= \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \kappa^2 + V \end{align} Multiplying both sides by $\hat{\Psi} e^{i\kappa x - i \omega t}$, and integrating, we get the expressions for the above derivatives. \begin{align} i\hbar\pdv{\Psi}{t} &= - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \pdv[2]{\Psi}{x} + V \Psi \label{main} \end{align} This is the Schrodinger equation.

The Wave Function

The Schrodinger Equation

Born's statistical interpretation gives that the probability of finding a particle between two points is the integral of $\Psi*\Psi = |\Psi|^2$ between them. Therefore $\Psi$ is normalized such that \begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\Psi|^2 \,dx &= 1 \\ \end{align}

Useful properties of $\Psi$

$\Psi$ and its derivatives tend to $0$ at $\pm \infty$. This gives the formula for integration by parts as \begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \Psi_1 \pdv{\Psi_2}{x} \, dx = -\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \pdv{\Psi_1}{x} \Psi_2 \, dx \end{align} Some useful expansions are \begin{align} -i \hbar \pdv{\Psi^*}{t} &= - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \pdv[2]{\Psi^*}{x} + V \Psi^* \\ \pdv{|\Psi|^2}{t} &= \frac{i\hbar}{2m} \pdv{x}\left(\Psi^*\pdv{\Psi}{x} - \pdv{\Psi^*}{x}\Psi\right) \\ \end{align} Adding a phase factor to a solution does not alter the probability distribution and is equivalent to adding a constant to the potential. \begin{align} \Psi' &= \Psi e^{\frac{-iV_0t}{\hbar}}\\ V' &= V + V_0 \end{align}

Probability

\begin{align} \langle f(x) \rangle &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)|\Psi|^2 \,dx \end{align} Let $\Psi_1$ and $\Psi_2$ be two wave functions satisfying the same potential \begin{align} &\dv{t} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \Psi_1^* \Psi_2 \,dx\\ &= \frac{1}{i\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} i \hbar \pdv{\Psi_1^*}{t} \Psi_2 + \Psi_1^* i \hbar \pdv{\Psi_2}{t}\,dx \\ &= \frac{1}{i\hbar}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \left(\Psi_1^* \pdv[2]{\Psi_2}{x} - \pdv[2]{\Psi_1^*}{x} \Psi_2 \right) \,dx \\ &= 0 \\ \end{align}

Probability Current

The probability of finding a particle in region $a < x < b$ over the course of time, can be imagined to have 'probability' flowing in at $x = a$ and flowing out at $x = b$ at rate $J$. \begin{align} \dv{P_{ab}}{t} &= \dv{t} \int_a^b |\Psi|^2 dx = J(a, t) - J(b, t) \\ J(x, t) &= \pdv{|\Psi|^2}{t} \\ \end{align}

Momentum

\begin{align} \dv{\langle x \rangle}{t} &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \pdv{|\Psi|^2}{t} \,dx \\ &= \frac{i\hbar}{2m}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \pdv{x}\left(\Psi^* \pdv{\Psi}{x} - \pdv{\Psi^*}{x}\Psi\right)\,dx \\ &= - \frac{i\hbar}{2m}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \Psi^* \pdv{\Psi}{x} - \pdv{\Psi^*}{x}\Psi \,dx \\ &= - \frac{i\hbar}{m}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \Psi^* \pdv{\Psi}{x} \,dx \\ \langle p \rangle &:= m \dv{\langle x \rangle}{t} = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \Psi^* \left(- i \hbar \pdv{x}\right) \Psi \,dx \end{align}

Ehrenfest's Theorem

\begin{align} \dv{\langle p \rangle}{t} &= - i \hbar \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \pdv{\Psi^*}{t} \pdv{\Psi}{x} + \Psi^* \pdv[2]{\Psi}{t}{x}\,dx \\ &= - i \hbar \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \pdv{\Psi^*}{t} \pdv{\Psi}{x} - \pdv{\Psi^*}{x} \pdv{\Psi}{t}\,dx \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} V \left(\Psi^* \pdv{\Psi}{x} + \Psi \pdv{\Psi^*}{x}\right)\,dx \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} - \pdv{V}{x} |\Psi|^2 \,dx \\ &= \left\langle - \pdv{V}{x} \right\rangle \end{align}

The Uncertainty Principle

\begin{align} \sigma_{f(x, p)} &= \langle f^2 \rangle - \langle f \rangle^2\\ \sigma_x \sigma_p &\geq \frac{\hbar}{2} \end{align}

Some Wave Functions

  • $\Psi(x, t) = Ae^{-\lambda |x|}$

    Potential: \begin{align*} V = - \lambda \delta(x) + \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \lambda^2 \end{align*} Normalisation: \begin{align*} A = \sqrt{\lambda} \end{align*} Expectations: \begin{align*} \langle x \rangle &= \frac{1}{2\lambda}\\ \langle x^2 \rangle &= \frac{1}{2\lambda^2}\\ \langle p \rangle &= 0\\ \langle p^2 \rangle &= \hbar^2 \lambda^2\\ \end{align*}

  • $\Psi(x, t) = A e^{-\lambda x^2}$

    Potential: \begin{align*} V = \frac{\hbar^2 \lambda}{m} (2\lambda x^2 - 1) \end{align*} Normalisation: \begin{align*} A = \sqrt[4]{\frac{2\lambda}{\pi}} \end{align*} Expectations: \begin{align*} \langle x \rangle &= 0 \\ \langle x^2 \rangle &= \frac{1}{4\lambda} \\ \langle p \rangle &= 0 \\ \langle p^2 \rangle &= \lambda \hbar^2 \end{align*}

Probability Distributions in Classical Systems

Let a particle of mass $m$ be in a potential well $V(x)$ between $x=a$ and $x=b$, with total energy $E$. Let $P$ be the probability distribution. The velocity of the particle is: \begin{align*} v(x) &= \sqrt{\frac{2(E - V)}{m}}\\ p(x) &= m v(x) \end{align*} The time taken to move between the endpoints is: \begin{align*} T = \int \,dt = \int_a^b \frac{1}{v(x)}\,dx \end{align*} \begin{align*} P(x)dx &= \frac{dt}{T} = \frac{dx}{|v(x)|T}\\ P(p)dp &= \frac{dt}{T} = \frac{dp}{\left|\dv{p}{t}\right|T}=\frac{dp}{\left|\dv{V}{x} \left(x^{-1}(p)\right)\right| T}\\ \end{align*} Simple Harmonic Oscillator: \begin{align*} V(x) &= \frac{1}{2}kx^2 \\ T &= \pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} \\ P(x) &= \frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{\frac{2E}{k} - x^2}} \\ P(p) &= \frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{2mE - p^2}} \\ \langle x \rangle &= \langle p \rangle = 0 \\ \langle x^2 \rangle &= \frac{E}{k} \\ \langle p^2 \rangle &= mE \\ \end{align*}

Unstable Particles

The total probability of finding a decaying particle must decrease with time, the only way this is possible is if potential has an imaginary component. Let $V_0$ be the true potential, then $V=V_0-i\Gamma$ gives an exponentially decreasing total probability. \begin{align*} P(t) &= \int_{\infty}^{\infty} \Psi^* \Psi \,dx \\ \dv{P}{t} &= \frac{1}{i\hbar} \int_{\infty}^{\infty} i\hbar \pdv{\Psi^*}{t} \Psi + \Psi^* i \hbar \pdv{\Psi}{t} \,dx \\ &= -\frac{2\Gamma}{\hbar} P \\ P &= e^{-\frac{2\Gamma t}{\hbar}} \\ \end{align*} Then the lifetime $\tau = \frac{\hbar}{2\Gamma}$ or $\Gamma=\frac{\hbar}{2\tau}$. This new imaginary potential is negligible for significant lifetimes.

Time Independent Schrodinger Equation

In this special case, $V$ is only a function of position and the partial differential equation becomes separable. \begin{align} \Psi(x, t) = \psi(x) \varphi(t) \\ \pdv{\Psi(x, t)}{t} = \psi(x) \dv{\varphi(t)}{t} \\ \pdv[2]{\Psi(x, t)}{x} = \dv[2]{\psi(x)}{x} \varphi(t) \\ \end{align} Substituting into \eqref{main} \begin{align} i \psi \dv{\varphi}{t} = - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \dv[2]{\psi} \varphi + V \psi \varphi\\ i \frac{1}{\varphi} \dv{\varphi}{t} = - \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{1}{\psi} \dv[2]{\psi} + V\\ \end{align} Note that the LHS is time dependent while RHS is position dependent. Hence, the equation must equal a constant $E$. The PDE has reduced to two ODEs. \begin{align} \dv{\varphi}{t} = - \frac{iE}{\hbar} \varphi \\ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\dv[2]{\psi}{x} + V \psi = E \psi \label{t-ind-main}\\ \end{align}
$E$ must be real and $>\min{V(x)}$ for solutions in Hilbert space.
Further, the above differential equation does not contain imaginary coefficients. Therefore, if $\psi$ is a solution, so is $\psi^*$ and we need only look at real solutions.
The second equation is the time independent Schrodinger equation. The first is a simple differential equation with solution $\varphi = e^{-\frac{iE}{\hbar}}$. Note that $\varphi$ does not influence the probability distribution and the probability distribution is constant in time (stationary state).

The second is contracted to the form $\hat{H}\psi = E \psi$ where $\hat{H}$ is the Hamiltonian operator. Because this is an eigenvalue problem on $\hat{H}$, the solution leads to the same energy $E$ for all observations. Each value of $E_n$ yields a different value of $\psi_n$, so there are infinite solutions.

Every solution of the time dependent Schrodinger equation with $V$ independent of $t$, can be written as a linear combination of $\psi_n \varphi_n$: \begin{align} \Psi = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n \psi_n e^{-\frac{iE_n}{\hbar}t} \end{align}
$|c_n|^2$ is the probability that a measurement of energy gives $E_n$. \begin{align} \langle H \rangle = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} |c_n|^2 E_n \end{align}
