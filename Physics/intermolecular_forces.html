Dipoles

Dipoles are point (almost) objects with no net charge but have an electric field and potential. A dipole is two charges of equal magnitude but opposite direction separated by a distance. We make observations from a large distance so that the dipole appears almost point like.

Consider two charges $q$ and $-q$ placed at $(a, 0)$ and $(-a, 0)$. The electric potential at $(x, y)$ is given by: \begin{align} V &= kq \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{(x-a)^2 + y^2}} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{(x+a)^2 + y^2}}\right) \\ &\approx kq \frac{2ax}{(x^2+y^2)^{\frac{3}{2}}} \end{align} We can define the dipole moment $\vec{p}:= 2qa \hat{x}$, then \begin{align} V = \frac{k \vec{p} \cdot \vec{r}}{r^3} \end{align}

Interaction between two dipoles

Consider two dipoles with dipole moments $\vec{p_1} = 2a_1q_1 \hat{x}$ and $\vec{p_2} = 2a_2q_2 (\cos{\theta} \hat{x} + \sin{\theta} \hat{y})$. Assume they are separated by a large distance $r \hat{x}$. The potential energy of the configuration is given by: \begin{align} U &= -q_2 V_1(\sqrt{(r - a_2 \cos{\theta})^2 + (a_2 \sin{\theta})^2}) + q_2 V_1(\sqrt{(r + a_2 \cos{\theta})^2 + (a_2 \sin{\theta})^2}) \\ &\approx \dv{V_1}{r} 4q_2a_2 \cos{\theta} \\ &= \frac{-2kp_1}{r^3} 2 p_2 \cos{\theta} \\ &= -4k \frac{\vec{p_1} \cdot \vec{p_2}}{r^3} \end{align}
